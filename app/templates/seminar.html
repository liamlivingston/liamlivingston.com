Current Problem

July 2023 was the hottest month recorded in the past 120,000 years (Moustafa et al. 2023). The escalation of global warming will rapidly increase risks, such as Extreme Weather Events (EWEs), to settlements in high-vulnerability areas, where approximately 3.3 to 3.6 billion people live, with coastal-specific climate hazards projected to put roughly a billion people at risk, according to the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPPC), a division of the United Nations (IPCC 2018)(IPPC 2022). Worsening global warming causes more frequent and severe EWEs, which affect low- and middle-income countries, coastal and low-lying areas, and the urban poor disproportionately (Hallegatte et al. 2017). This paper will focus on the urban poor of Metro Manila in the Philippines, who meet these criteria very closely. Survivors of typhoons, floods, and other EWEs have shown a wide range of psychological symptoms, including anxiety, depression, and PTSD, with displaced victims reporting the symptoms to a greater extent compared to non-displaced victims. Similarly, in animals, threats on their life, i.e., predators, and the fear of them, have been shown to cause PTSD-like changes in the brains of wild animals (Zanette et al. 2019). By reducing scenarios where victims of EWEs feel that their life is threatened, PTSD and other psychological effects associated with such encounters will drop. This leads to my research question: Should places at an elevated risk to EWEs, that are increasing in count and severity due to anthropogenic climate change, such as Metro Manila in the Philippines, adopt priorities of action of the Sendai Framework, an international framework on Disaster Risk Reduction, in order to lower the injury, mortality, and displacement rate of EWEs, to address the growing mental health crisis? We will first discuss the psychological effects of increasing EWEs as a result of climate change, including PTSD, then current studies on PTSD intervention, and finally, the Sendai Framework and its potential implementation.

Research



Background

In the brackish tidal marshes of Chesapeake Bay, an ecosystem similar to the Palo Alto Baylands, sea level rise, a disturbance to the ecosystem, caused an impact on the organic matter decay rates there (Kirwan et al., 2013).

Picture of the area that was observed 4 (Livingston, 2022).

Claim

Sea level rise is a disturbance that would likely impact the observed ecosystem in the Palo Alto Baylands, causing a reduced population of species.

Evidence

Research shows that salt marshes are threatened by sea level rise as shown in a previous statement. In addition, by the end of the century, ~30%-~75% of marshes could drown, with the figures being higher for other continents (Crosby et al., 2016).. Finally, research shows that sea levels will likely be from 0.61m-1.10m following a RCP8.5 model (I.P.C.C., 2022).

Reasoning

Sea level rise is a disturbance that will likely impact the Palo Alto Baylands ecosystem because of rising sea levels, sea level rise’s effect on organic matter decay, and salt water marshes that could drown as a result of this. Evidence shows that salt water marshes are threatened by sea level rise. Additionally, evidence shown above shows how sea level rise is likely to reach 0.61m-1.10m by 2100. In addition, marshes are expected to drown from ~30%-~75% by the end of the century due to sea level rise as stated previously. This is all important because salt water marshes drowning could result in a reduction of producers such as african daisies, yellow forsythia, algae, and golden thyme that primary consumers rely on. This would cause a chain reaction up the energy pyramid resulting in an ecosystem falling apart. I believe that this ecosystem is bottom-up control because of how the consumers are reliant on the producers.
